Recursiveness, Emergence, and Iteration in Kant

Kant's concept of public reason, encapsulated in the maxim "sapere aude" or "dare to know," reveals patterns of emergence and iteration that echo Christopher Kelty's "recursive publics." This "dare to know" is not a simple, linear progression, but rather a continuous, iterative process of self-reflection, public engagement, and the ongoing re-evaluation of principles—a spiral rather than a scroll.

We are all intersectional, relational beings, and Kant understood that enlightenment emerges not from isolated individuals, but from the complex interplay between our private roles and our public voice. Like color mixing—where distinct hues combine to create new shades that cannot be understood by their components alone—enlightenment emerges from the recursive interaction between thought and action, self and society.

Kant's Recursive Principles

The Categorical Imperative as Iteration

For Kant, moral reasoning involves iterating through a recursive self-test: individuals must ask themselves if they would be content for their maxim to hold good as a universal law for everyone, including themselves. This is not a one-time calculation but an ongoing practice—each moral decision requires returning to first principles, spiraling through the test again. Like the simple spiral that gains meaning through repetition, the categorical imperative gains force through continuous re-application.

Publicity as Emergent Property

Kant introduces the "transcendental formula of public right": "All actions relating to the rights of other men are wrong, if the maxims from which they follow are inconsistent with publicity." Justice emerges as a property of the system when principles can withstand public scrutiny. Like complex systems where the whole cannot be understood by examining parts alone, moral legitimacy emerges only when individual maxims meet the test of universal public reason.

Enlightenment as Continuous Emergence

Kant defines enlightenment as deliverance from prejudice and superstition, achieved by learning to think for oneself. This ongoing process of critical self-reflection is inherently recursive—like emergence in complex systems, where new properties arise from interactions rather than being predetermined. Each generation must spiral through this process anew, with enlightenment emerging from the collective practice of public reason rather than being handed down from authorities.

The Internet as Recursive Public

Christopher Kelty's concept of a "recursive public" provides a modern analogue to this spiral of public reason. A recursive public is defined by its active concern with "the material and practical maintenance and modification of the technical, legal, practical, and conceptual means of its own existence as a public."

Continuous Iteration of Infrastructure

Just as Kant's moral agent iterates through testing maxims against universal law, a recursive public continuously rebuilds its infrastructure—from software, protocols, and standards to legal documents. This mirrors Kant's call for self-legislation, but on a collective, technologically mediated scale. The iteration never stops; each cycle builds upon the last.

Modifiability and Emergence

A key aspect of recursive publics is modifiability—the ability to transform, improve, and redistribute content. This questions the very notion of "finality" in knowledge or cultural products. Like color mixing where new hues emerge from combinations, new meanings and possibilities emerge from the continuous remixing and modification of shared resources. Knowledge becomes "living and constantly changing" rather than static, with understanding emerging through iteration rather than declaration.

In essence, Kelty's "recursive public" on the Internet extends Kant's philosophical framework of self-governance into the material and technical realm. The Internet, as an infrastructure that can be inhabited and transformed by its users, provides a concrete manifestation of the spiral of public reason, where "the conditions of freedom" are not merely discussed but actively constructed and continually redefined through emergence and iteration.

What Is Enlightenment?

Immanuel Kant (1784) - Modern Rendering

Enlightenment is humanity's emergence from self-incurred immaturity—a habit of intellectual dependence.

This dependence is the inability to use one's own judgment without another's guidance. It is self-incurred not from lack of intelligence, but from lack of courage and resolve to think independently.

Dare to think for yourself. Have the courage to use your own understanding. That is the motto of enlightenment.

Laziness and fear explain why many, long after they are capable of independent thought, remain dependent—and why others so easily set themselves up as their guides.

Dependence is comfortable. If search engines serve as my memory, recommendation feeds shape my opinions, advisors manage my choices—then I need not think. I only subscribe; others will gladly do the hard work for me.

Those who pose as guardians first make followers passive, then warn them of the "dangers" of walking alone. Yet the danger is small. After a few falls, we learn to walk. Such warnings merely frighten people away from further attempts.

It is hard for anyone to work free of dependence once it feels like second nature. One grows fond of it and becomes temporarily unable to judge for oneself, because one has rarely been encouraged to try.

Rules and formulas—mechanical substitutes for genuine thought—become chains of permanent dependence.

Those who break free still make uncertain leaps over small ditches, unaccustomed to free movement. Only a few, by cultivating their own minds, secure their freedom and chart their own course.

That the public should enlighten itself is more likely; indeed, if granted freedom, enlightenment is almost inevitable.

Even among entrenched authorities, a few always think for themselves. Having thrown off the yoke, they spread a spirit of critical appreciation for each person's calling to think.

But when a dependent public is stirred by those incapable of enlightenment, it can force even the guardians to remain yoked. Prejudices take revenge on their originators.

A public attains enlightenment only gradually. Revolutions may topple tyrants and profiteers, but they do not reform a manner of thinking. New prejudices replace old ones and leash the unthinking many.

Nothing is required for enlightenment except freedom—the most harmless kind: the freedom to use reason publicly on all matters.

Everywhere we hear: "Do not question." "Do not question—follow orders." "Do not question—pay." "Do not question—believe." "Do not question—keep scrolling."

Which restrictions hinder enlightenment, and which might even advance it?

The public use of reason must always be free; only this brings enlightenment. The private use of reason—in a role or office—may often be narrowly restricted without blocking progress.

By public use, I mean the use anyone makes of reason as a thinker before the whole world. By private use, I mean the reasoning one exercises within a professional post bound by protocol.

A soldier on duty must obey; yet as a citizen and writer, he may publicly examine military policy. A taxpayer must pay; yet as a thinker, she may argue that a levy is unjust.

A pastor or employee must teach or implement what a symbol or policy requires; yet as a scholar addressing the world, that same person may freely propose reform. For society's "guardians" to be unthinking themselves would be an absurdity that perpetuates absurdities.

May institutions bind themselves—and us—by unalterable rules that forbid public questioning and reform for a lifetime?

They should not. Such designs aim to preclude the further enlightenment of humankind. One generation must not conspire to deny the next the chance to correct errors and extend knowledge.

Provisional arrangements may be tolerated, provided citizens—especially educators, journalists, technologists—retain the freedom to critique and improve them in public.

Leaders ought to ensure that proposed changes keep civil order, while allowing people to pursue their intellectual and spiritual well-being. Their role is not to command belief, but to prevent coercion among citizens. Rule is diminished, not strengthened, when it suppresses public reasoning.

Do we live in an enlightened age?

No. But we may live in an age of enlightenment.

Much is still lacking for people to apply understanding confidently without external guidance. Yet paths are opening, and the obstacles to release from self-incurred immaturity are gradually diminishing.

A leader who refuses to prescribe what people must believe or read, and instead protects their freedom to reason in public—such a leader is enlightened.

Under such protection, professionals may fulfill their duties while also speaking openly, as thinkers, to the world—even in frank disagreement with prevailing symbols or policies.

This spirit of freedom spreads, even where it struggles. It proves by example that freedom need not endanger public order, but can actually strengthen it through the cultivation of independent thought.
